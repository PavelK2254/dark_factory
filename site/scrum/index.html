A two-minute introduction to Scrum

Scrum is a lightweight framework for delivering complex products through short, inspect-and-adapt cycles. This page summarises the parts that matter most.

What Scrum is

Scrum is a lightweight framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products. It is built on empirical process control: progress comes from transparency, regular inspection, and timely adaptation. Scrum does not prescribe how to engineer a product; it provides the structure within which teams learn quickly and adjust. For the canonical definition, see the Scrum Guide.

When it helps

Scrum is most useful when work is complex — the requirements, the technology, or both are likely to change as the team learns. It creates fast feedback loops so teams can adjust direction. It is not a guarantee of success, and it is a poor fit for purely repetitive or fully predictable work, which other approaches handle better.

Visual summary

Accountabilities (roles)

Product Owner

Maximises the value of the product by managing the Product Backlog and ordering its items.

Scrum Master

Establishes Scrum and helps the team and organisation be effective with it.

Developers

Create a usable Increment each Sprint that meets the Definition of Done.

Events

Sprint

A fixed-length container (usually one to four weeks) in which all other events occur and an Increment is produced.

Sprint Planning

The team agrees a Sprint Goal and selects the work it believes can be done to meet it.

Daily Scrum

A short check-in for Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adjust the plan.

Sprint Review

The team and stakeholders inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog based on what was learned.

Sprint Retrospective

The team inspects how the Sprint went and decides on improvements for the next one.

Artifacts

Product Backlog

An ordered list of what is needed to improve the product. Its commitment is the Product Goal.

Sprint Backlog

The Sprint Goal plus the items selected for the Sprint and a plan for delivering them.

Increment

A concrete step toward the Product Goal. Its quality commitment is the Definition of Done.

The Sprint

The Sprint is the heartbeat of Scrum: a time-boxed period — typically one to four weeks — that contains all other events. Its outcome is a usable Increment that meets the Definition of Done. A new Sprint starts immediately after the previous one ends, keeping work moving and feedback frequent.

Common myths

  • “Scrum is a methodology with fixed scope.” Scrum is a framework, not a methodology, and scope is expected to evolve as the team learns.
  • “The Daily Scrum is a status report to a manager.” It is a planning event for Developers to adjust their work toward the Sprint Goal.
  • “The Scrum Master assigns tasks to the team.” Developers self-manage who does what; the Scrum Master coaches and removes impediments.
  • “Sprints can be extended if work is not finished.” The Sprint length is fixed; unfinished items return to the Product Backlog.

Frequently asked questions

How does Scrum relate to Agile?

Agile is a set of values and principles. Scrum is one framework that helps teams work in line with those values, but it is not the only one.

What is the difference from Kanban at a glance?

Scrum organises work into time-boxed Sprints with defined events and accountabilities. Kanban focuses on visualising flow and limiting work in progress, without prescribing roles or fixed iterations. Teams sometimes combine both.

Who decides what gets built?

The Product Owner is accountable for the Product Backlog and decides what is most valuable next. Stakeholders provide input; Developers decide how much can be done in a Sprint.

How long should a Sprint be?

One month or less. Many teams choose two weeks. Shorter Sprints give faster feedback; longer Sprints carry more risk between inspections.
