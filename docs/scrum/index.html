What Scrum is

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps teams deliver value through iterative work, frequent inspection, and adaptation. It defines a small set of accountabilities, events, and artifacts — and leaves the details of how the work gets done to the team.

When it helps

Scrum fits complex problems where requirements and solutions are uncertain and need to be discovered through delivery. It helps when a team needs a steady cadence to ship something useful, learn from it, and adjust — rather than committing up front to a fixed plan.

Roles (accountabilities)

A Scrum Team has three accountabilities — together they own delivering value each Sprint.

Product Owner

Accountable for maximizing the value of the product by managing the Product Backlog.

Scrum Master

Accountable for the team's effectiveness and for establishing Scrum as defined in the Scrum Guide.

Developers

Accountable for creating a usable Increment that meets the Definition of Done every Sprint.

Events

Five time-boxed events create regularity and remove the need for other meetings.

Sprint

A fixed, time-boxed container (one month or less) inside which all the other Scrum events happen and an Increment is created.

Sprint Planning

Initiates the Sprint by defining why it is valuable, what can be done, and how the chosen work will get done.

Daily Scrum

A 15-minute event for Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the plan for the next day.

Sprint Review

Inspects the outcome of the Sprint with stakeholders and determines future adaptations to the Product Backlog.

Sprint Retrospective

Closes the Sprint by planning ways to increase quality and effectiveness in how the team works together.

Artifacts

Each artifact represents work or value, and each has a commitment that gives it focus.

Product Backlog

An ordered list of what is needed to improve the product. Its commitment is the Product Goal.

Sprint Backlog

The plan by and for the Developers — the Sprint Goal, the selected Product Backlog items, and how to deliver them.

Increment

A concrete step toward the Product Goal. Each Increment is additive to all prior Increments and must be usable.

Definition of Done

The shared standard of quality an Increment must meet to be considered complete and releasable.

The Sprint: a time-boxed container

The Sprint is the heartbeat of Scrum. It is a fixed-length cycle, one month or less, during which the team turns ideas into a usable Increment. Sprint Planning opens it, the Daily Scrum keeps it on track, and the Sprint Review and Retrospective close it — same length, same rhythm, every time.

Myths

A few common misconceptions, set straight against the Scrum Guide.

Scrum is not a methodology with fixed scope agreed up front

Scrum is a lightweight framework, not a fixed methodology. The Product Backlog is ordered and refined as the team learns; changing requirements are expected, not avoided.

The Scrum Master is not a project manager who assigns tasks

The Scrum Master is a true leader who serves the Scrum Team and the wider organization. Developers self-manage how the Sprint work gets done.

The Daily Scrum is not a status meeting for managers

It is a 15-minute event for the Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the plan for the next day of work.

FAQ

Short answers to questions that come up most often.

How does Scrum relate to Agile?

Agile is the broader mindset described by the Agile Manifesto's values and principles. Scrum is one specific lightweight framework that embodies Agile. Using Scrum well means living those values, not just running the events.

How is Scrum different from Kanban at a glance?

Scrum uses fixed-length Sprints, three defined accountabilities, and a small set of events to inspect and adapt. Kanban focuses on visualizing flow and limiting work in progress, without prescribed roles or time-boxed iterations. Many teams blend ideas from both.

Who decides what is built?

The Product Owner. They are accountable for ordering the Product Backlog and maximizing the value of the product. Developers decide how to build the selected work; stakeholders inform decisions but do not set the order.

Can Scrum be used outside software?

Yes. Scrum is intentionally lightweight and applies to any complex work where empirical process control — transparency, inspection, and adaptation — helps a team deliver value.
